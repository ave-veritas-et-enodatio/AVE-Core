    \qquad R_y \equiv \frac{\alpha^2 m_e c^2}{2}
    = 13.606\;\text{eV}
    \label{eq:E0_eigenvalue}
\end{equation}
This is the \textbf{Op6 eigenvalue} of a soliton
(Axiom~4) in a $1/r$ Coulomb cavity (Axiom~2),
derived from energy minimisation.  The mode number
$n$ is the standing wave winding number on the
flux ring (Eq.~\ref{eq:standing_wave_ring}).

\subparagraph{(h) Angular and radial mode decomposition
(Axiom~1, 3D lattice).}

The derivation in (e)--(g) treats the electron as
a ring with all its phase accumulated \emph{azimuthally}
(around the orbit).  On the 3D lattice (Axiom~1),
a soliton in a central potential has \textbf{two
independent standing wave conditions}:

\begin{center}
\small
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
\begin{tabular}{llll}
\hline
\textbf{Mode} & \textbf{AVE definition} &
\textbf{Winding \#} & \textbf{QM name} \\
\hline
Angular & Phase per orbit around the ring &
$l$ & Angular momentum $L = l\hbar$ \\
Radial & Phase per bounce between turning points &
$n_r$ & Radial quantum number \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
The \textbf{total quantum number} is the sum:
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{n = n_r + l + 1}
    \label{eq:n_decomposition}
\end{equation}
The $+1$ arises because the radial wavefunction
has a half-wavelength at each turning point
(the boundary condition imposes $\pi/2$ phase
at the inner and outer classical turning points).

\noindent\textbf{Physical meaning of $l$:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $l = n - 1$, $n_r = 0$ (circular ring):
          All energy in azimuthal circulation.
          Fixed radius $R = n^2 a_0 / Z$.
          This is the case derived in (e)--(g).
    \item $l = 0$, $n_r = n - 1$ (radial breathing mode):
          No angular momentum ($L = 0$).
          The soliton oscillates \textbf{radially}
          between inner and outer turning points.
          No circulating current---a spherically
          symmetric pulsation of the lattice.
    \item $0 < l < n-1$ (mixed):
          Both angular circulation and radial
          bouncing.  Elliptical orbit.
\end{itemize}

\noindent\textbf{The Coulomb $l$-degeneracy.}
For the pure $1/r$ potential (Axiom~2), the energy
depends \emph{only} on the total $n$, not on how it
splits between $l$ and $n_r$:
\begin{equation}
    E_{0,n} = \frac{Z^2 R_y}{n^2}
    \qquad\text{for all $l = 0, 1, \ldots, n{-}1$}
    \label{eq:l_degeneracy}
\end{equation}
This degeneracy is exact for a single electron
and is a consequence of the $1/r$ potential's
special symmetry.  It breaks in multi-electron
atoms because the orbit shape (determined by $l$)
controls how deeply the outer electron
penetrates an inner shell---see Stage~E2.

\noindent\textbf{Why $l$ matters for IE:}
An $l = 0$ electron passes through all inner
shells on every radial oscillation, seeing the
full nuclear charge $Z$ part of the time.
An $l = n{-}1$ electron stays at fixed $R$,
outside the inner shells.  The orbit-averaged
enclosed charge $\sigma$ (E2) therefore depends
on $l$: lower $l \to$ more penetration $\to$
smaller $\sigma \to$ higher $Z_{\text{net}} \to$
stronger binding $\to$ higher IE.

\begin{resultbox}{Step 1 Summary}
A single electron in a nuclear Coulomb field is a
current ring soliton (Axioms~1, 4) with winding
number~$n$.  Energy minimisation gives:\\
$R_n = n^2 a_0 / Z$, \quad
$v_n = Z\alpha c / n$, \quad
$\omega_n = Z^2 \alpha^2 m_e c^2 / (\hbar n^3)$, \quad
$E_{0,n} = Z^2 R_y / n^2$.\\
No wavefunctions.  No Schr\"odinger equation.
All from Axioms~1, 2, 4 and the standing wave topology.
\end{resultbox}

\paragraph{Step 2: Lattice Strain Between Shells (Axiom~4).}

When two or more electrons occupy different shells ($R_a < R_b$),
there is a region of vacuum lattice between them.  Before computing
inter-shell coupling (Step~3), it must be determined whether the
lattice in this region is modified from its free-space state.

\subparagraph{(a) Strain sources.}
Three fields contribute to the lattice strain between
$R_a$ and $R_b$:
\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\hline
\textbf{Source} & \textbf{Field type} & \textbf{Frequency} \\
\hline
Nuclear charge $+Ze$ & Coulomb $E = Ze/(4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2)$
    & DC (static) \\
Inner electron $-e$ at $R_a$ & Coulomb (orbit-averaged)
    & DC (time-averaged) \\
Inner electron oscillation & Magnetic dipole $B \propto R_a^2/r^3$
    & AC at $\omega_a$ \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subparagraph{(b) DC strain from nuclear Coulomb field.}
In Axiom~4, the strain amplitude $A$ is the local field
magnitude normalised to the saturation (yield) field:
\begin{equation}
    A(r) \equiv \frac{|E(r)|}{E_{\text{yield}}}
    \label{eq:A_def}
\end{equation}
The yield field $E_{\text{yield}}$ is a \textbf{universal}
lattice constant---the field strength at which the lattice
saturates ($S \to 0$).  It is set by the proton confinement
surface: a single proton ($Z = 1$) at $r = D_p \approx 0.88$~fm
reaches saturation, so:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\text{yield}} = \frac{e}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 D_p^2}
    \qquad\text{(universal, from $Z = 1$ proton)}
\end{equation}
A nucleus with charge $Ze$ produces Coulomb field
$E(r) = Ze/(4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2)$.  Therefore:
\begin{equation}
    A_{\text{nuc}}(r)
    = \frac{E(r)}{E_{\text{yield}}}
    = \frac{Ze/(4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2)}{e/(4\pi\varepsilon_0 D_p^2)}
    = Z\!\left(\frac{D_p}{r}\right)^{\!2}
    \label{eq:A_nuclear}
\end{equation}
Note: this is a \textbf{field ratio} ($\propto 1/r^2$),
distinct from Op4's pairwise strain $A = d_{\text{sat}}/r$
($\propto 1/r$), which is a geometric confinement ratio
for two point-like nodes.  The nuclear Coulomb field produces
$1/r^2$ strain because $E \propto 1/r^2$.

At the Bohr radius ($r = a_0 = 5.29 \times 10^{-11}$~m):
\begin{equation}
    A_{\text{nuc}}(a_0)
    = Z\!\left(\frac{0.88\times 10^{-15}}
    {5.29\times 10^{-11}}\right)^{\!2}
    = Z \times 2.8 \times 10^{-10}
\end{equation}
For all atoms ($Z \leq 118$):
$A_{\text{nuc}} < 3.3 \times 10^{-8}$.
This is \textbf{deep Regime~I}
($A \ll 1$, $S \approx 1$).

\subparagraph{(c) AC strain from inner-shell oscillation.}
The inner electron at $R_a$ oscillates at frequency $\omega_a$.
As a current ring, it produces a near-field magnetic
dipole that falls off as $(R_a / r)^3$.

At a Hopf link crossing (two rings at the same radius),
the local strain reaches $A = p_c / (2\sqrt{2}) \approx 0.42$
(derived from the packing fraction geometry, $p_c = 8\pi\alpha$;
see Ch.~\ref{ch:topological_matter}).
At distance $R_b$ from the inner ring, the dipole falloff gives:
\begin{equation}
    A_{\text{AC}}(R_b) \approx 0.42
    \times \left(\frac{R_a}{R_b}\right)^{\!3}
    \label{eq:A_ac_dipole}
\end{equation}
For Li ($R_a/R_b = 1/4$):
$A_{\text{AC}} \approx 0.42 \times 0.016 = 0.007$.
This is Regime~I.  For Be ($R_a/R_b = 1/4$): same ratio.

\subparagraph{(d) Impedance invariance and wave speed (Axiom~4).}
Axiom~4 gives the saturated capacitance per node:
$C_{\text{eff}} = C_0 / \sqrt{1 - A^2}$.
Since $C \propto \varepsilon$, the effective permittivity is:
\begin{equation}
    \varepsilon_{\text{eff}}
    = \frac{\varepsilon_0}{\sqrt{1 - A^2}}
\end{equation}
Symmetric saturation (the lattice is isotropic---$\mu$ and
$\varepsilon$ scale identically under strain):
\begin{equation}
    \mu_{\text{eff}}
    = \frac{\mu_0}{\sqrt{1 - A^2}}
\end{equation}
The impedance ratio:
\begin{equation}
    Z = \sqrt{\frac{\mu_{\text{eff}}}{\varepsilon_{\text{eff}}}}
    = \sqrt{\frac{\mu_0 / \sqrt{1-A^2}}
    {\varepsilon_0 / \sqrt{1-A^2}}}
    = \sqrt{\frac{\mu_0}{\varepsilon_0}}
    = Z_0 = 377\;\Omega
    \label{eq:Z0_invariant}
\end{equation}
The saturation factors cancel: $Z_0$ is \textbf{exactly}
invariant under symmetric saturation, for all $A$.

The local wave speed is \textbf{not} invariant:
\begin{equation}
    c_{\text{eff}}
    = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_{\text{eff}}\,\varepsilon_{\text{eff}}}}
    = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0\,\varepsilon_0 / (1-A^2)}}
    = c_0\,\sqrt{1 - A^2}
    \;\xrightarrow{A \ll 1}\;
    c_0\!\left(1 - \frac{A^2}{2}\right)
    \label{eq:c_eff}
\end{equation}
With $A \sim Z \times 10^{-10}$ from sub-step~(b),
the fractional correction is
$\Delta c / c \sim A^2/2 \sim 10^{-16}$---negligible
for all atoms.

\subparagraph{(e) Standing wave resonances?}
Could the inner electron's AC oscillation at $\omega_a$ create
standing waves in the inter-shell gap?  From Step~1,
the orbital frequency for $n = 1$, $Z = 1$ is
$\omega_1 = \alpha^2 m_e c^2 / \hbar$.
The corresponding electromagnetic wavelength:
\begin{equation}
    \lambda_{\text{EM}}
    = \frac{2\pi c}{\omega_1}
    = \frac{2\pi \hbar c}{\alpha^2 m_e c^2}
    = \frac{2\pi \hbar}{\alpha^2 m_e c}
    = \frac{2\pi a_0}{\alpha}
    \approx 860\,a_0
\end{equation}
using $a_0 = \hbar / (\alpha m_e c)$ from Step~1(f).
The inter-shell gap is $R_b - R_a \sim a_0$.  Since
$\lambda_{\text{EM}} \gg R_b - R_a$, no standing wave
resonances are possible in the gap.  The lattice responds
quasi-statically to the inner electron's oscillation.
\subparagraph{(f) Lattice supercavitation and the $Z = 1/\alpha$ limit.}

The electron defect has a saturated core ($S \to 0$:
a ``liquid cavity'' in the lattice).  As it orbits at velocity
$v = Z\alpha c / n$, the lattice must deform and reform behind
the moving defect.  The relevant speed for cavity closure is the vacuum
\textbf{shear wave speed} $c_S$.

\paragraph{Wave speed assignment (Axiom~1).}
Photons are \textbf{transverse} (shear) waves on the $\mathcal{M}_A$ lattice
(Vol~I, Chapter~1).  The canonical identification is therefore:
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{c_S \equiv c}
    \label{eq:c_shear}
\end{equation}
The speed of light \emph{is} the shear wave speed.  From this and the
vacuum Poisson ratio $\nu = 2/7$ (Axiom~3), the P-wave (longitudinal)
speed follows via the standard elastodynamic relation:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{c_P^2}{c_S^2} = \frac{2(1-\nu)}{1-2\nu}
    = \frac{2(1-2/7)}{1-4/7}
    = \frac{10/7}{3/7}
    = \frac{10}{3}
    \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad
    c_P = c\sqrt{\frac{10}{3}} \approx 1.826\,c
    \label{eq:cp_from_nu}
\end{equation}
The P-wave speed exceeds $c$.
This is physically consistent: pure longitudinal compression carries
no extractable energy in a Möbius-chirality lattice---precisely the
result derived for the entanglement thread (Vol~I, \S3.7,
no-signalling theorem).  The $c_P > c$ mode is a
\textit{phase-information channel}, not an energy channel, and does
not violate Einsteinian causality.

\noindent\textbf{Cavitation criterion.}
The electron's orbital velocity is $v = Z\alpha c / n$.
When $v > c_S$, the lattice cannot close behind the
moving defect---the cavity elongates into a
\textbf{supercavitation wake}, distorting the defect geometry.

Because $c_S = c$, the cavitation number simplifies:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{C} \equiv \frac{v}{c_S}
    = \frac{Z\alpha c / n}{c}
    = \frac{Z\alpha}{n}
    \label{eq:cavitation_number}
\end{equation}
This is strikingly clean: the cavitation number is the orbital velocity in natural units ($v/c = Z\alpha/n$).

\begin{itemize}
\item $\mathcal{C} \ll 1$ (light atoms, $Z \lesssim 40$):
      The lattice reforms quasi-statically behind the defect.
      The electron ring maintains its equilibrium toroidal
      shape.  The lattice is ``passive''---standard
      $E_0 = Z^2 R_y / n^2$ holds without correction.
\item $0.3 \lesssim \mathcal{C} \lesssim 0.7$ (heavy atoms, $Z \approx 50$--$100$):
      The electron's orbital velocity is a significant fraction
      of the lattice shear speed $c$.  The wake does not fully
      cavitate, but the lattice strain grows nonlinearly,
      distorting the defect geometry---changing $L$, $Z_{LC}$,

      ``relativistic corrections'' (spin-orbit, Darwin term)

      continuously rather than switching on abruptly.
\item $\mathcal{C} \to 1$ ($Z \to 1/\alpha \approx 137$):
      The electron's orbital speed approaches the lattice
      shear wave speed $c$.  No bound state can
      exist beyond this limit---the defect would have to outrun
      its own medium.  This is the AVE derivation of the
      \textbf{maximum atomic number} $Z_{\max} = 1/\alpha$.
\end{itemize}

All computed at bare nuclear charge $Z$.

\paragraph{Stage C: Coupling Admittance (from Universal Operators).}

\noindent\textbf{Regime identification.}
At atomic scales, $r \sim a_0 \gg \ell_{\text{node}} = d_{\text{sat}}$.
The strain amplitude is $A = d_{\text{sat}}/r \approx \alpha/(2\pi)
\approx 10^{-3}$---deep in Regime~I (linear).
Op4 (\texttt{universal\_pairwise\_energy}) reduces to:
\begin{equation}
    U(r) = -\frac{K}{r}\,(T^2 - \Gamma^2)
    \xrightarrow[\Gamma \to 0]{A \ll 1}
    -\frac{K}{r}
    \qquad \text{(pure Coulomb)}
\end{equation}
with $K = \alpha\hbar c$ for electron-electron coupling (Axiom~2).
The only places where $A \sim 1$ (saturation activates) are at
the Hopf link crossings, where Op2 provides the $p_c/2$ correction.

\subparagraph{Type 1: Same-shell Hopf link (Op4 + Op2).}
Two flux rings on the same orbit ($R_i = n^2 a_0 / Z$) form
a Hopf link.  At antipodal separation $r = 2R_i$, Op4 in
Regime~I gives a Coulomb coupling:
\begin{equation}
    V_{\text{Hopf}} = \frac{\alpha\hbar c}{2 R_i}
    = \frac{\alpha\hbar c \cdot Z}{2 n^2 a_0}
    = \frac{Z \cdot \alpha^2 m_e c^2}{2 n^2}
    = \frac{Z \cdot R_y}{n^2}
\end{equation}
using $a_0 = \hbar/(\alpha m_e c)$ and $R_y = \alpha^2 m_e c^2/2$.
Normalising by the single-electron eigenvalue $E_0 = Z^2 R_y/n^2$:
\begin{equation}
    k_{\text{bare}} = \frac{V_{\text{Hopf}}}{E_0}
    = \frac{Z R_y / n^2}{Z^2 R_y / n^2} = \frac{1}{Z}
\end{equation}
Each Hopf link has two crossings.  At each crossing, the strain
saturates and Op2 (\texttt{universal\_\allowbreak saturation}) reduces the
transmitted power by $p_c/2$ (two crossings $\times$ $p_c/4$ each).
Including both electrons in the pair (factor of 2):
\begin{equation}
    k_{\text{Hopf}} = \frac{2}{Z}\left(1 - \frac{p_c}{2}\right),
    \qquad y_{\text{Hopf}} = \frac{k_{\text{Hopf}}}{Z_{LC,i}}
    \label{eq:y_hopf}
\end{equation}
The $p_c/2$ factor is Op2 (\texttt{universal\_saturation}) at the
two Hopf link crossings where the strain locally saturates.

\subparagraph{Type 2: Same-shell orthogonal (Op4 + Op2, $90^\circ$).}
Two flux rings in different $m_l$ orbitals. Their tori cross
at $90^\circ$:
\begin{equation}
    k_\perp = k_{\text{Hopf}} \times \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}},
    \qquad y_\perp = \frac{k_\perp}{Z_{LC,i}}
    \label{eq:y_perp}
\end{equation}

\subparagraph{Type 3: Cross-shell concentric rings (Op4 only, no Op2).}
Two flux rings at radii $R_a < R_b$, coplanar. Their tori do
\emph{not} intersect ($A \ll 1$ everywhere along the path between
them), so Op2 does not activate---no crossing correction.

The orbit-averaged Op4 potential between two charges on
concentric circular orbits at $R_a$ and $R_b$ is the
time average of $U = -\alpha\hbar c / r_{12}(\theta)$
over all relative orbital angles.  This is a standard
electrostatic integral:
\begin{equation}
    \langle V_{ab} \rangle
    = \frac{\alpha\hbar c}{\pi R_b}
    \int_0^\pi
    \frac{d\theta}{\sqrt{1 + (R_a/R_b)^2 - 2(R_a/R_b)\cos\theta}}
    = \frac{2\alpha\hbar c}{\pi R_b}\,
    K\!\left(\frac{R_a}{R_b}\right)
    \label{eq:ring_coulomb}
\end{equation}
where $K(m)$ is the complete elliptic integral of the first kind.

In the coupled resonator framework (Op5), the coupling
coefficient is the mutual energy normalised to the geometric
mean of the node eigenvalues---this is the standard
admittance-normalised coupling used in the Y-matrix:\begin{equation}
    k_{\text{cross}} = \frac{\langle V_{ab} \rangle}
    {\sqrt{E_{0,a} \times E_{0,b}}}, \qquad
    E_{0,i} \equiv \frac{Z^2 R_y}{n_i^2}
    \;\;\text{(Op6, uncoupled)}
    \label{eq:k_cross}
\end{equation}
The cross-shell mutual admittance:
\begin{equation}
    y_{\text{cross}} = \frac{k_{\text{cross}}}
    {\sqrt{Z_{LC,a} \times Z_{LC,b}}}
    \label{eq:y_cross}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Stage D: Y-Matrix Assembly (Axiom~1, Kirchhoff).}

Each electron is a node on the LC lattice (Axiom~1).
Kirchhoff's current law (KCL) at node $i$ states:
the sum of all currents entering node $i$ equals zero.

At node $i$, two types of current flow:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Self-current.}  The node draws current
          through its own impedance $Z_{LC,i}$:
          \begin{equation}
              I_{\text{self},i} = \frac{V_i}{Z_{LC,i}}
          \end{equation}
    \item \textbf{Mutual current.}  Current flows between
          nodes $i$ and $j$ through their pairwise Coulomb
          coupling (Op4), with mutual admittance
          $y_{ij} = k_{ij}/Z_{LC}$ (Stage~C):
          \begin{equation}
              I_{ij} = (V_i - V_j)\,y_{ij}
          \end{equation}
\end{itemize}
KCL at node $i$:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{V_i}{Z_{LC,i}}
    + \sum_{j \neq i} (V_i - V_j)\,y_{ij}
    = I_{\text{ext},i}
\end{equation}
Collecting terms in $V_i$ and $V_j$:
\begin{equation}
    V_i \!\left[\frac{1}{Z_{LC,i}}
    + \sum_{j \neq i} y_{ij}\right]
    - \sum_{j \neq i} V_j\,y_{ij}
    = I_{\text{ext},i}
\end{equation}
Reading off the $N \times N$ admittance matrix $[Y]$
where $\mathbf{I} = [Y]\,\mathbf{V}$:
\begin{align}
    Y_{ii} &= \frac{1}{Z_{LC,i}}
    + \sum_{j \neq i} y_{ij}
    \qquad \text{(self + all couplings)} \\
    Y_{ij} &= -y_{ij}
    \qquad \text{(negative mutual admittance)}
\end{align}
This is Kirchhoff's current law on the discrete LC
lattice (Axiom~1), with Op4 providing the pairwise
admittances.  No wavefunctions, no Hamiltonian matrix.

\paragraph{Stage E: Hierarchical Mode Analysis.}

The energy extraction follows a two-level hierarchy:

\subparagraph{(E1) Same-shell bonding mode.}
Within each shell of principal quantum number $n$, all $N_n$
electrons are identical LC resonators at frequency
$\omega_n$ with pairwise coupling $k_n$ (Hopf or orthogonal,
from Stage~C).  For shells where all pairs share the
same $k$ (e.g.\ $s$-shells), the coupling graph is the
complete graph $K_{N_n}$; for mixed-coupling shells
($p$-shell), see the weighted graph analysis below.

\noindent\textbf{Derivation from Stage~D.}
For $N$ identical nodes, the Y-matrix from Stage~D has
self-admittance $Y_{ii} = 1/Z_{LC} + (N{-}1)\,y$ and
mutual $Y_{ij} = -y$, with $y = k/Z_{LC}$.
This matrix can be written:
\begin{equation}
    [Y] = \left(\frac{1}{Z_{LC}} + Ny\right)\,\mathbf{I}
    \;-\; y\,\mathbf{J}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{I}$ is the identity and $\mathbf{J}$
is the all-ones matrix.  The eigenvalues of $\mathbf{J}$
are $N$ (eigenvector: all in-phase) and $0$ ($N{-}1$ fold
degenerate: out-of-phase modes).

The eigenvalues of $[Y]$ are therefore:
\begin{align}
    \lambda_{\text{bond}} &= \frac{1}{Z_{LC}} + Ny - yN
    = \frac{1}{Z_{LC}}
    \qquad\text{(bonding: all in-phase)} \\
    \lambda_{\text{anti}} &= \frac{1}{Z_{LC}} + Ny
    \qquad\text{(antibonding: $N{-}1$ degenerate)}
\end{align}
The bonding mode has the \textbf{lowest admittance}
(highest impedance)---it is the mode where all $N$
current rings oscillate in phase, and the mutual
coupling effectively increases the inertia of each node.

In LC terms: the in-phase mode sees an effective
capacitance $C_{\text{eff}} = C_0(1 + k(N{-}1))$
(all mutual capacitances add in parallel), while the
inductance $L_0$ is unchanged.  Therefore:
\begin{equation}
    \omega_{\text{bond}}
    = \frac{1}{\sqrt{L_0\,C_{\text{eff}}}}
    = \frac{\omega_0}{\sqrt{1 + k(N{-}1)}}
    \label{eq:omega_bond_derived}
\end{equation}
The bonding mode energy follows from the virial
theorem and the standing wave condition.
From Step~1: $\omega = n\hbar/(m_e R^2)$,
so $R^2 = n\hbar/(m_e\omega)$.
The virial theorem gives
$E = -\tfrac{1}{2}m_e v^2 = -\tfrac{1}{2}m_e\omega^2 R^2$,
substituting $R^2$:
\begin{equation}
    E = -\tfrac{1}{2}m_e\omega^2 \cdot \frac{n\hbar}{m_e\omega}
    = -\tfrac{1}{2}n\hbar\omega
    \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad
    \frac{E_{\text{bond}}}{E_0}
    = \frac{\omega_{\text{bond}}}{\omega_0}
    = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + k(N{-}1)}}
\end{equation}
Therefore:
\begin{equation}
    E_{n,\text{bond}} = \frac{Z^2 R_y / n^2}
    {\sqrt{1 + k_n(N_n - 1)}}
    \label{eq:bonding_mode}
\end{equation}
where $k_n$ is the per-pair coupling coefficient for shell $n$.
The antibonding modes are unoccupied
(they represent an excited configuration).

\textit{Verification (He):} $Z=2$, $n=1$, $N_1 = 2$,
$k_1 = (2/2)(1 - p_c/2) = 0.9083$:
\begin{equation}

    \quad
    E_{\text{total}}(2) = 2 \times 39.40 = 78.79\,\text{eV}
\end{equation}
$\text{IE} = 78.79 - 54.42 = 24.37\,\text{eV}$. \checkmark

\noindent\textbf{3.\;The impedance step at $R_a$

The inner shell's enclosed charge creates a step

\begin{equation}
    V(r) = \begin{cases}
    -Z\,\alpha\hbar c\,/\,r, & r < R_a

    -(Z - N_a)\,\alpha\hbar c\,/\,r, & r > R_a
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:V_step}
\end{equation}
This step in $V$ produces a step in $k(r)$---and
therefore a step in the soliton's effective impedance:
\begin{align}
    k_{\text{in}}(R_a)
    &= \frac{\sqrt{2m_e(E + Z\alpha\hbar c / R_a)}}
    {\hbar}
    \qquad\text{(higher $k$: tighter winding)} \\
    k_{\text{out}}(R_a)
    &= \frac{\sqrt{2m_e(E + (Z{-}N_a)\alpha\hbar c / R_a)}}
    {\hbar}
    \qquad\text{(lower $k$: looser winding)}
\end{align}
The ratio $k_{\text{in}} / k_{\text{out}}$ is the
radial impedance mismatch at the shell boundary.

\noindent\textbf{4.\;Reflection and transmission at the
boundary (Op5).}

reflects and partially transmits.  The reflection
coefficient follows from standard transmission line
theory (the \emph{same} Op5 used for the Y-matrix):
\begin{equation}
    \Gamma = \frac{k_{\text{out}} - k_{\text{in}}}
    {k_{\text{out}} + k_{\text{in}}}
    \label{eq:reflection_coeff}
\end{equation}
Since $k_{\text{in}} > k_{\text{out}}$
(higher potential inside $\to$ higher wavenumber),
$\Gamma < 0$: the reflected wave inverts.
The transmitted fraction carries the penetrating
amplitude into the high-$Z$ inner region.

\noindent\textbf{5.\;The eigenvalue condition (Op6).}
The soliton standing wave must satisfy constructive
interference after all reflections.
The total phase accumulated is:
\begin{equation}

    {\text{inner region phase}}
    \;+\;
    \underbrace{\int_{R_a}^{r_{\max}} k_{\text{out}}(r)\,dr}_
    {\text{outer region phase}}
    \;+\;
    \underbrace{\phi_\Gamma}_
    {\text{reflection phase at } R_a}
    = n_r \pi
    \label{eq:radial_eigenvalue}
\end{equation}
where $n_r = n - l - 1$ is the number of radial
nodes (Step~1(h)).  This phase-matching condition IS
Op6: the eigenvalue $E$ is the value for which the
total phase equals $n_r \pi$.

\begin{resultbox}{The Atom as a Radial Waveguide}
The multi-electron atom is a \textbf{radial waveguide}
on the vacuum LC lattice (Axiom~1).  The electron
soliton (Axiom~4) propagates through a passive
Regime~I medium at $Z_0 = 377\;\Omega$.\\[4pt]
The nuclear charge (Axiom~2) and inner electron shells
(Op4) create a position-dependent wavenumber $k(r)$.
Inner shells produce impedance steps where $k$ changes
discontinuously.  The soliton wave partially reflects
at each step (Op5).\\[4pt]
The bound state is the standing wave satisfying
$\lambda_{\min}(S^\dagger S) \to 0$ (Op6)---phase
matching across all radial regions.  This is the
\textbf{same operator chain}
(Op4 $\to$ Op5 $\to$ Op6) validated at nuclear,
antenna, and galactic scales.\\[4pt]
The $l = 0$ orbit has no centrifugal barrier and
penetrates all inner shells, acquiring larger
inner-region phase $\to$ stronger binding $\to$
higher IE.  The $l$-dependent splitting of
multi-electron atoms is a direct consequence of this

\subparagraph{(E2d) Radial eigenvalue solver specification.}

The preceding sections derived the analytical framework:
the atom is a radial waveguide (E2b-iii), the
$l = 0$ orbit penetrates inner shells (E2a-ii),
and the classical orbit time-average underestimates
the effect (E2b, Assessment).  The full solution
requires the \textbf{radial eigenvalue solver}---which
uses the same universal operators validated at
nuclear, protein, and antenna scales.

\noindent\textbf{Input:}
$Z$ (nuclear charge), $n$ (total quantum number),
$l$ (angular winding number), and a list of inner
shell boundaries $\{(R_a, N_a)\}$ where $R_a$ is
the shell radius and $N_a$ the electron count.

\noindent\textbf{Output:}
$E$ (eigenvalue energy in eV)---the energy of the
$(n, l)$ electron in the multi-electron atom.

\noindent\textbf{Algorithm (5 steps, all from axioms):}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Build the piece-wise radial potential
$V_{\text{eff}}(r)$.}
From Axiom~2 (Coulomb) and Axiom~1 (angular momentum):\vspace{-4pt}
\begin{equation}
    V_{\text{eff}}(r) = -\frac{Z_{\text{net}}(r)\,
    \alpha\hbar c}{r}
    + \frac{l^2\hbar^2}{2\,m_e\,r^2}
    \label{eq:V_eff_radial}
\end{equation}
where $Z_{\text{net}}(r) = Z$ for $r$ inside all
inner shells, and decreases by $N_a$ at each
shell boundary $R_a$ (Gauss's law, Axiom~2).

\item \textbf{Local wavenumber $k(r)$.}
From Axiom~4 (soliton mass) and energy conservation:\vspace{-4pt}
\begin{equation}
    k(r) = \frac{\sqrt{2\,m_e(E - V_{\text{eff}}(r))}}
    {\hbar}
    \qquad [\text{m}^{-1}]
    \label{eq:k_local}
\end{equation}
Real in classically allowed regions ($E > V_{\text{eff}}$),
imaginary in forbidden regions (evanescent tail).

\item \textbf{Phase integral in each region.}
Between consecutive shell boundaries, the phase
accumulated by the soliton standing wave is the
Sommerfeld integral:\vspace{-4pt}
\begin{equation}
    \phi_i = \int_{r_{i}}^{r_{i+1}} k(r)\,dr
    \qquad [\text{rad}]
    \label{eq:phase_integral}
\end{equation}
For a pure $-Z_{\text{net}}\alpha\hbar c / r
+ l^2\hbar^2/(2m_e r^2)$ potential, the closed form
is:\vspace{-4pt}
\begin{align}
    \phi_i &= \left[
    \sqrt{-2m_e E}\;r
    \;\sqrt{1 + \frac{Z_{\text{net}}\alpha\hbar c}{E\,r}
    - \frac{l^2\hbar^2}{2m_e E\,r^2}}
    \right]_{r_i}^{r_{i+1}} \nonumber \\
    &\quad + Z_{\text{net}}\alpha
    \sqrt{\frac{m_e}{-2E}}\,
    \arcsin\!\left(\frac{2Er + Z_{\text{net}}
    \alpha\hbar c}{\sqrt{Z_{\text{net}}^2\alpha^2\hbar^2 c^2
    + 2El^2\hbar^2/m_e}}\right)
    \Bigg|_{r_i}^{r_{i+1}}
\end{align}
This is evaluable in closed form---no numerical
quadrature required.

\item \textbf{Reflection at each shell boundary (Op3/Op5).}
At each $R_a$, the wavenumber jumps because
$Z_{\text{net}}$ changes.  The reflection coefficient
(Eq.~\ref{eq:reflection_coeff}):\vspace{-4pt}
\begin{equation}
    \Gamma_a = \frac{k_{\text{out}}(R_a)
    - k_{\text{in}}(R_a)}
    {k_{\text{out}}(R_a) + k_{\text{in}}(R_a)}
\end{equation}
This is \texttt{universal\_reflection()}
from the physics engine (Op3)---the
\textbf{same operator} used for nuclear binding,
protein folding, and antenna matching.
The reflection phase: $\phi_{\Gamma,a}
= \arg(\Gamma_a)$.

\item \textbf{Eigenvalue condition (Op6).}
The total phase must satisfy the radial standing
wave condition:\vspace{-4pt}
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\sum_i \phi_i + \sum_a \phi_{\Gamma,a}
    = n_r\,\pi}
    \qquad n_r = n - l - 1
    \label{eq:radial_eigenvalue_condition}
\end{equation}
Find $E$ such that this holds.  This is Op6
(\texttt{universal\_eigenvalue\_target}),
implemented via Newton--Raphson root-finding
(\texttt{eigenvalue\_root\_finder.find\_eigenstate}
---both already in the physics engine).
\end{enumerate}

\noindent\textbf{Eigenvalue bracket (Axiom~2).}
The total phase $\phi(E)$ is a monotonically
\emph{increasing} function of the orbit size,
which is a monotonically \emph{decreasing}
function of binding energy $E$.  Therefore:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $E_{\text{hi}} = Z^2 R_y / n^2$
          (bare Coulomb, no screening):
          orbit is small, phase $< n_r\pi$.
    \item $E_{\text{lo}} = (Z - N_{\text{inner}})^2
          R_y / n^2$ (full Gauss screening):
          orbit is large, phase $> n_r\pi$.
\end{itemize}
Both bounds are from Axiom~2 (Coulomb coupling
with and without enclosed charge).  The true
eigenvalue---with partial penetration---lies
uniquely between $E_{\text{lo}}$ and $E_{\text{hi}}$.
For Li~$2s$: $E_{\text{hi}} = 30.6$~eV,
$E_{\text{lo}} = 3.4$~eV; experiment $= 5.39$~eV.

\subparagraph{(E2d-ii) ABCD transfer matrix cascade
(Op1, Op3, Op5, Op6).}

The phase integral
(Eq.~\ref{eq:radial_eigenvalue_condition}) is a
WKB approximation: it captures the \emph{phase}
accumulated in each region but neglects the
\emph{amplitude} change at each impedance step.
For strong steps (e.g.\ $Z_{\text{net}}$ dropping
from 3 to 1 at the $1s$ boundary), amplitude
effects are significant.

The correct AVE treatment uses the \textbf{ABCD
transfer matrix}---the same cascade tool used in
the nuclear solver (flux tube sections) and the
antenna solver (coaxial feed sections).

\noindent\textbf{Setup (derivation from axioms).}
The soliton field amplitude $\psi(r)$ on the radial
transmission line satisfies an equation derived
entirely from the axioms:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Axiom~4} (soliton dispersion):
          $E = \hbar^2 k^2/(2m_e) + V$, giving
          $k^2(r) = 2m_e(E - V(r))/\hbar^2$.
    \item \textbf{Axiom~2} (Coulomb):
          $V_{\text{Coulomb}} = -Z_{\text{net}}\,
          \alpha\hbar c / r$.
    \item \textbf{Axiom~1} (angular standing wave):
          the angular winding number $l$ produces a
          centrifugal term
          $V_{\text{centrifugal}} = l(l+1)\hbar^2
          /(2m_e r^2)$, derived from
          $L^2 = l(l+1)\hbar^2$ on the discrete
          lattice.
\end{itemize}
Substituting into $\psi'' + k^2 \psi = 0$:
\begin{equation}
    \psi''(r) + \left[
    \frac{2m_e}{\hbar^2}
    \left(E + \frac{Z_{\text{net}}\,\alpha\hbar c}{r}\right)
    - \frac{l(l+1)}{r^2}\right]\psi(r) = 0
    \label{eq:radial_wave}
\end{equation}

\noindent\textbf{AVE vocabulary:}
\begin{center}
\small
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\hline
\textbf{Symbol} & \textbf{AVE meaning} \\
\hline
$\psi(r)$ & Soliton field amplitude along the
    radial TL \\
$f_l(r)$  & Forward-propagating wave (regular at
    $r \to 0$) \\
$g_l(r)$  & Backward-propagating wave (irregular) \\
$l(l+1)$  & Angular winding eigenvalue (Axiom~1
    lattice) \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
Equation~(\ref{eq:radial_wave}) has two linearly
independent solutions $f_l(r)$ and $g_l(r)$.
These are the forward and backward propagating
waves on the radial transmission line.

\noindent\textbf{ABCD matrix per section.}
In region $i$ (charge $Z_{\text{net},i}$), any
solution is a linear combination
$\psi = \alpha\,f_l + \beta\,g_l$.  The transfer
matrix propagates $(\psi, \psi')$ from $r_1$ to
$r_2$:
\begin{equation}
    \begin{pmatrix} \psi(r_2) \\ \psi'(r_2)
    \end{pmatrix}
    = \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} A & B \\ C & D
    \end{pmatrix}}_{\text{ABCD}_i}
    \begin{pmatrix} \psi(r_1) \\ \psi'(r_1)
    \end{pmatrix}
    \label{eq:abcd_section}
\end{equation}
The elements are given by the Wronskian
$W = f_l g_l' - f_l' g_l$:
\begin{align}
    A &= \frac{f_l(r_2)\,g_l'(r_1) -
         g_l(r_2)\,f_l'(r_1)}{W} \nonumber \\
    B &= \frac{g_l(r_2)\,f_l(r_1) -
         f_l(r_2)\,g_l(r_1)}{W} \nonumber \\
    C &= \frac{f_l'(r_2)\,g_l'(r_1) -
         g_l'(r_2)\,f_l'(r_1)}{W} \nonumber \\
    D &= \frac{g_l'(r_2)\,f_l(r_1) -
         f_l'(r_2)\,g_l(r_1)}{W}
    \label{eq:abcd_elements}
\end{align}
These contain zero free parameters---only the
solutions to Eq.~(\ref{eq:radial_wave}), which
are themselves determined by the axioms.

\noindent\textbf{Junction matching (Op3).}
At each shell boundary $R_a$, the soliton field and
its derivative are continuous---the lattice does not
break at the boundary, so there is no discontinuity
in $\psi$ or $\psi'$:
\begin{equation}
    \psi_{\text{in}}(R_a) = \psi_{\text{out}}(R_a),
    \qquad
    \psi'_{\text{in}}(R_a) = \psi'_{\text{out}}(R_a)
    \label{eq:junction_continuity}
\end{equation}
However, $Z_{\text{net}}$ changes at $R_a$
(Axiom~2, Gauss's law), so the basis solutions
$(f_l, g_l)$ change.  The junction matrix converts
from one basis to the other using
Eq.~(\ref{eq:junction_continuity}).

The local impedance of each section is
$Z_{\text{TL}} = \hbar k / m_e$
(Op1, \texttt{universal\_impedance}),
where $k$ is the wavenumber at $R_a$.  The
reflection coefficient at the junction is
(Op3, \texttt{universal\_reflection}):
\begin{equation}
    \Gamma_a = \frac{Z_{\text{out}} -
    Z_{\text{in}}}{Z_{\text{out}} +
    Z_{\text{in}}} = \frac{k_{\text{out}} -
    k_{\text{in}}}{k_{\text{out}} +
    k_{\text{in}}}
    \label{eq:junction_gamma}
\end{equation}
This is the same operator used at nuclear
saturation boundaries and antenna apertures.

\noindent\textbf{Cascade (Op5).}
The total transfer matrix is the product of all
section and junction matrices:
\begin{equation}
    \text{ABCD}_{\text{total}} =
    \text{ABCD}_{\text{outer}} \times
    \text{junction}(R_a) \times
    \text{ABCD}_{\text{inner}} \times \cdots
    \label{eq:abcd_cascade}
\end{equation}
This is the radial version of Op5
(\texttt{universal\_ymatrix\_to\_s}): cascading
impedance sections to get the total scattering
behaviour.

\noindent\textbf{Eigenvalue condition (Op6).}
The bound state requires:
\begin{itemize}
    \item At $r \to 0$: $\psi$ finite
          (select the regular solution $f_l$
          in the innermost region).
    \item At $r \to \infty$: $\psi \to 0$
          (select the exponentially decaying
          solution in the outermost region).
\end{itemize}
The inner boundary condition fixes $\psi(r_0)
= f_l(r_0)$, $\psi'(r_0) = f_l'(r_0)$.  After
propagating through the ABCD cascade, the outer
field is:
\[
    \psi_{\text{out}} = A_{\text{total}}\,f_l(r_0)
    + B_{\text{total}}\,f_l'(r_0)
\]
The decaying solution at $r \to \infty$
corresponds to $B_{\text{total}} = 0$
(the irregular component vanishes).  Therefore:
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{B_{\text{total}}(E) = 0}
    \label{eq:abcd_eigenvalue}
\end{equation}
Root-find $E$ using the Axiom~2 bracket
($E_{\text{lo}} = (Z-N_{\text{inner}})^2 R_y/n^2$
to $E_{\text{hi}} = Z^2 R_y/n^2$) and Brent's
method.

\subparagraph{(E2e) Non-penetrating orbits
($l \geq 1$, Problem~4).}

\noindent\textbf{AVE vocabulary:}
\emph{s-winding} $= l{=}0$ (radial oscillation,
no angular nodes); \emph{p-winding} $= l{=}1$
(one angular node); \emph{d-winding} $= l{=}2$
(two angular nodes).

When $l \geq 1$, the angular standing wave
(Axiom~1) produces a centrifugal barrier.  From
Step~1(h), the angular eigenvalue on the discrete
lattice is $L^2 = l(l+1)\hbar^2$, giving:
\begin{equation}
    V_{\text{centrifugal}}(r) =
    \frac{l(l+1)\hbar^2}{2m_e\,r^2}
    \label{eq:centrifugal_barrier}
\end{equation}
This barrier diverges as $r \to 0$, creating a
classical turning point at:
\begin{equation}
    r_{\text{turn}} \approx
    \frac{l(l+1)\hbar^2}{2m_e\,
    Z_{\text{net}}\alpha\hbar c}
    = \frac{l(l+1)}{2}\,\frac{a_0}{Z_{\text{net}}}
    \label{eq:centrifugal_turning}
\end{equation}
For $l \geq 1$ and typical shell radii
$R_a = a_0 n_a^2 / Z$, this turning point lies
\emph{outside} the inner shell boundary:
$r_{\text{turn}} > R_a$.  The soliton never
reaches the boundary---it reflects off the
centrifugal wall first.  There is no impedance step
to cross.

The energy is therefore Problem~1 with
Gauss-screened charge (Axiom~2):
\begin{equation}
    E_{l \geq 1} = \frac{(Z - \sigma)^2\,R_y}{n^2}
    \label{eq:E_screened}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma$ is the Op4 orbit-averaged enclosed
charge (Eq.~\ref{eq:sigma_op4}).  No transfer
matrix is needed---the soliton never crosses the
boundary.

\subparagraph{(E2f) Graded impedance taper (Axiom~2).}

The step-function $Z_{\text{net}}(r)$ used in
E2d models the $1s$ shell as a \emph{sharp
impedance junction}.  But the $1s$ solitons
($l{=}0$) oscillate radially---their charge is
distributed over a range, not localised at a
single radius.  On the radial transmission line,
this creates a \textbf{graded impedance taper},
not a step.

\noindent\textbf{1. Taper vs.\ step (TL theory).}
In transmission-line engineering, a sharp impedance
step gives strong reflection
(Op3, $\Gamma = (Z_2 - Z_1)/(Z_2 + Z_1)$).
A graded taper over many wavelengths gives
\emph{weak} reflection---the wave transmits
through with little disturbance (Klopfenstein
taper, antenna matching).

The $1s$ charge distribution extends from
$r \approx 0$ to $r \approx a_0$.
The characteristic impedance $Z_{\text{TL}}(r)
= \hbar k(r)/m_e$ transitions smoothly across
this range.  The taper length $\Delta r \sim a_0$
is comparable to the $2s$ soliton's wavelength
$\lambda_{2s} \sim a_0$, so the reflection is
\emph{reduced} but not zero.

\noindent\textbf{2. Inner-shell charge profile.}
Each $1s$ soliton has a radial density from the
solution to Eq.~(\ref{eq:radial_wave}) with
$Z_{\text{eff}} = Z$ (the unperturbed nuclear
charge---no imported parameters):
\begin{equation}
    \rho_{1s}(r) \propto r^2\,
    e^{-2Z\,r/a_0}
    \label{eq:1s_density}
\end{equation}
The enclosed charge fraction at radius $r$ is
(Axiom~2, Gauss's law on the soliton density):
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{1s}(r) = 1 - e^{-2Zr/a_0}
    \left(1 + \frac{2Zr}{a_0}
    + \frac{2Z^2 r^2}{a_0^2}\right)
    \label{eq:smooth_screening}
\end{equation}
At $R_a = a_0/3$ with $Z = 3$:
$\sigma_{1s} = 0.323$---only 32\% of the
$1s$ charge is enclosed, not~100\%.

\noindent\textbf{3. Tapered cascade (Op5).}
The graded taper is modelled as $N_{\text{sec}}$
thin ABCD sections (E2d-ii), each with a constant
$Z_{\text{net},i}$ sampled from
Eq.~(\ref{eq:smooth_screening}):
\begin{equation}
    Z_{\text{net}}(r_i) = Z
    - N_a \times \sigma_{1s}(r_i)
    \label{eq:z_net_taper}
\end{equation}
The total transfer matrix is the product
(Eq.~\ref{eq:abcd_cascade}):
\begin{equation}
    \text{ABCD}_{\text{total}} =
    \prod_{i=1}^{N_{\text{sec}}}
    \text{ABCD}_i(r_i, r_{i+1},
    Z_{\text{net},i})
\end{equation}
The eigenvalue condition $B_{\text{total}}(E) = 0$
(Eq.~\ref{eq:abcd_eigenvalue}) is unchanged.

\noindent\textbf{4. Physical effect.}
The distributed taper gives weaker net reflection
than the sharp step.  The $2s$ soliton passes
through the $1s$ region more freely, requiring a
larger effective orbit to satisfy the standing-wave
condition.  This \emph{reduces} the binding energy,
shifting the Li ionisation energy toward the
experimental value.

\noindent\textbf{5. Architecture preserved.}
The atom remains a cascaded transmission line
(Problem~3).  The same universal operators apply:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Op1: $Z_{\text{TL}} = \hbar k / m_e$
          in each thin section.
    \item Op3: distributed reflection
          $\Gamma \ll (Z_2 - Z_1) / (Z_2 + Z_1)$
          due to grading.
    \item Op5: ABCD cascade of all sections.
    \item Op6: eigenvalue from
          $B_{\text{total}} = 0$.
\end{itemize}

\noindent\textbf{6. Connection to standard theory.}
Axioms~1, 2, and~4 together reproduce the
standard radial wave equation
(Eq.~\ref{eq:radial_wave}) at Regime~I scales.
This is a \emph{result} of the AVE framework,
not an assumption: the lattice wave equation
(Axiom~1) with Coulomb potential (Axiom~2) and

\subparagraph{(E2g) Two-solver architecture
and the 3.5\% systematic.}

\noindent\textbf{1. Why the Y-matrix fails for cross-shell $l{=}0$.}
The coupled-resonator Y-matrix (Problem~2) models each
electron as a \emph{ring} at radius $R = n^2 a_0 /Z$.
The cross-shell coupling $k_{12}$ is computed from Op4
as the orbit-averaged Coulomb integral between concentric
rings (Eq.~\ref{eq:sigma_op4}).  For the
$1s \leftrightarrow 2s$ pair, this gives $k_{12} = 0.358$.

But the $2s$ soliton ($l{=}0$) is \emph{not} a ring---it
oscillates radially.  It \emph{penetrates through} the
$1s$ shell during each oscillation.  The ring--ring Op4
geometry overestimates the coupling because it assumes
the $2s$ charge is concentrated at a single radius $R_b$,
when in fact it is distributed from $r \approx 0$ to
$r \gg R_b$.

\noindent\textbf{2. Why the ABCD cascade succeeds.}
The ABCD cascade (E2d-ii, E2f) solves the radial wave
equation directly on the transmission line.  The $2s$
soliton's radial oscillation is captured exactly by the
ODE (Eq.~\ref{eq:radial_wave}).  The graded impedance
taper encodes the $1s$ charge distribution at each radius
(Eq.~\ref{eq:z_net_taper}).  This correctly handles
core penetration: the $2s$ soliton sees $Z \approx 3$
near the nucleus and $Z \approx 1$ far out, with the
transition governed by the $1s$ density profile.

\noindent\textbf{3. The correct decomposition.}
For any atom with inner-shell penetration ($l{=}0$):
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Solve the inner shell as a coupled resonator
          (Problem~2), giving $E_{\text{inner}}$.
    \item Solve the outer $l{=}0$ electron as an ABCD
          cascade through the graded taper
          (Problem~3), giving $E_{\text{outer}}$.
    \item The ionisation energy is $E_{\text{outer}}$
          directly (the inner shell remains bound
          in both the neutral atom and the ion).
\end{enumerate}
The two problems are solved \emph{independently}---the
inner-shell energy does not appear in the IE.  The
coupling between them enters only through the taper
profile $\sigma_{1s}(r)$, which is determined by the
inner-shell density.

\noindent\textbf{4. Remaining 3.5\% systematic.}
The graded taper treats the $1s$ pair as a \emph{passive
charge distribution}.  In reality, the $1s$ solitons are
coupled resonators that can \emph{polarise} in response
to the $2s$ soliton's passage.  This back-reaction
(dynamic polarisation of the $1s$ pair) is a
higher-order coupling between Problems~2 and~3:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The $1s$ pair is a resonant stub on the
          $2s$ transmission line.
    \item Its resonant frequency ($\sim 245$~eV)
          is far detuned from the $2s$ energy
          ($\sim 5$~eV): the frequency ratio
          $\omega_{2s}/\omega_{1s} = 1/8$.
    \item The large detuning ensures the stub
          loading is a small perturbation,
          consistent with the observed 3.5\%.
    \item The \emph{exact} correction requires
          solving the coupled 3-soliton problem
          on the lattice---a multi-body extension
          of the single-soliton ABCD cascade.
\end{itemize}

\subparagraph{(E2h) Lattice fluid mechanics
and the coupled-soliton problem.}

The radial wave equation (Eq.~\ref{eq:radial_wave}),
derived from Axioms~1, 2, 4, has a second
interpretation via the \textbf{Madelung
transformation}.  Write $\psi = \sqrt{\rho}\;
e^{iS/\hbar}$, where $\rho = |\psi|^2$ is the
soliton field intensity and
$v = \nabla S / m_e$ is the phase-gradient
velocity.  The wave equation splits into two
real equations:

\noindent\textbf{1. Continuity (charge conservation):}
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}
    + \nabla \cdot (\rho\,\mathbf{v}) = 0
    \label{eq:madelung_continuity}
\end{equation}

\noindent\textbf{2. Euler equation (lattice momentum):}
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial \mathbf{v}}{\partial t}
    + (\mathbf{v} \cdot \nabla)\mathbf{v}
    = -\frac{\nabla V}{m_e}
    - \frac{\nabla Q}{m_e}
    \label{eq:madelung_euler}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
    Q = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m_e}
    \frac{\nabla^2 \sqrt{\rho}}{\sqrt{\rho}}
    \label{eq:lattice_pressure}
\end{equation}
is the \textbf{lattice dispersion pressure}.

\noindent\textbf{3. Axiom mapping.}
\begin{center}
\small
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\hline
\textbf{Fluid quantity}
& \textbf{AVE origin} \\
\hline
Density $\rho$ & Soliton field $|\psi|^2$
    (Axiom~4) \\
Velocity $\mathbf{v}$
    & Phase gradient $\nabla S / m_e$
    (Axiom~4) \\
Pressure $Q$
    & Lattice dispersion (Axiom~1) \\
External force $-\nabla V$
    & Coulomb potential (Axiom~2) \\
Vortex
    & Topological soliton (electron) \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
The lattice pressure $Q$
(Eq.~\ref{eq:lattice_pressure}) arises from
Axiom~1: the discrete vacuum lattice resists
compression of the soliton field.  It has
no counterpart in classical fluid theory---it
is the AVE-native term that distinguishes
soliton mechanics from ordinary hydrodynamics.

\noindent\textbf{4. The coupled-soliton problem.}
The $N$-electron atom is $N$ coupled vortices
in the vacuum lattice fluid.  Each vortex
creates a $1/r$ flow field (Axiom~2, Coulomb).
The nonlinear term
$(\mathbf{v} \cdot \nabla)\mathbf{v}$
in Eq.~(\ref{eq:madelung_euler}) couples all
vortices simultaneously.

The ABCD cascade (E2d-ii, E2f) solves the
\emph{linearised} problem: one vortex (the $2s$
soliton) propagates through the static field
of the others (the $1s$ taper).  The 3.5\%
systematic is the \emph{nonlinear advection
correction}---the back-reaction of the $2s$
flow field on the $1s$ vortices.

\noindent\textbf{5. Self-consistent iteration.}
The steady-state ($\partial/\partial t = 0$)
solution is found by iterating the lattice
Euler equation:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Solve the $1s$ density
          $\rho_{1s}(r)$ from the ODE
          (Eq.~\ref{eq:radial_wave}).
    \item Compute
          $\sigma_{1s}(r) = \int_0^r
          \rho_{1s}\,4\pi r'^2\,dr'$
          (numerical Gauss integral,
          Axiom~2).
    \item ABCD cascade for the $2s$ soliton
          with $Z_{\text{net}}(r) = Z -
          N_a\,\sigma_{1s}(r)$.
    \item Extract $\rho_{2s}(r)$ from the
          $2s$ solution.
    \item Update $1s$ potential:
          $V_{1s}(r) = -Z\,\alpha\hbar c/r
          + \sigma_{2s}(r)\,\alpha\hbar c/r
          + \sigma_{1s,\text{other}}(r)\,
          \alpha\hbar c/r$.
    \item Re-solve $1s$ density, re-run
          ABCD cascade.
    \item Iterate until
          $|\Delta E_{2s}| < 0.001$~eV.
\end{enumerate}
This uses \textbf{zero new physics}---the same
ODE, the same ABCD cascade, and the same
Axiom~2 Coulomb potential.  The iteration is
the standard technique for solving steady-state
fluid equations: update the flow field,
re-solve the velocity, repeat.

\subparagraph{(E2i) Op2 crossing correction for
penetrating orbits.}

The ABCD cascade (E2f) treats the $1s$ pair
as a passive charge distribution but ignores
the \textbf{topological crossing} between the
$2s$ soliton and the $1s$ solitons at the
shell-penetration radius.

\noindent\textbf{1. Same-shell reference.}
For the $1s$ Hopf-linked pair (E1), Op2
modifies the coupling coefficient:
$k_{\text{Hopf}} = (2/Z)(1 - P_C/2)$,
where $P_C = 8\pi\alpha$ is the lattice
packing fraction (Axiom~3).  This Op2
correction accounts for the repulsive
topological energy at each crossing of the
Hopf link.

\noindent\textbf{2. Cross-shell l=0 crossings.}
The $2s$ soliton ($l{=}0$) oscillates radially
through the $1s$ shell.  On each radial period
it passes through the $1s$ region \emph{twice}
(once inward, once outward).  Only the
\textbf{same-phase} $1s$ dipole string contributes
a topological crossing (the anti-phase
soliton is orthogonal on the lattice and
does not cross).

For Li ($1s^2\,2s^1$) with one same-phase
inner string:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Number of same-phase crossings
          per period: $n_{\text{cross}} = 2$.
    \item Each crossing carries the same
          Op2 cost $P_C$ as the Hopf link.
\end{itemize}

\noindent\textbf{3. Crossing correction.}
The Op2 repulsive energy from the $2s$
soliton crossing one same-phase inner string
is:
\begin{equation}
    \delta E_{\text{Op2}} =
    E_{\text{ABCD}} \times
    \frac{P_C}{4}
    = E_{\text{ABCD}} \times 2\pi\alpha
    \label{eq:op2_cross_shell}
\end{equation}
The factor $1/4$ arises from:
$P_C/2$ per crossing at a Hopf-link vertex
(same as E1), divided by 2 because only one
out of two inner strings shares the $2s$
phase.

For Li:
\begin{equation}
    \delta E_{\text{Op2}} = 5.58 \times
    \frac{8\pi\alpha}{4}
    = 5.58 \times 0.0459
    = 0.256\;\text{eV}
\end{equation}
Corrected ionisation energy:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\text{IE}} = E_{\text{ABCD}} -
    \delta E_{\text{Op2}}
    = 5.58 - 0.26 = 5.32\;\text{eV}
\end{equation}

\subparagraph{(E2j) Same-shell interactions as
coupled transmission lines.}

The ABCD cascade (E2d-ii, E2f) models inner-shell
screening via Gauss's law: the $1s$ charge
distribution reduces the effective nuclear charge
seen by the $2s$ soliton.  This works because the
$1s$ and $2s$ shells are \textbf{spatially separated}
--- the $1s$ density is concentrated at
$r \approx a_0/Z$, while the $2s$ orbit extends to
$r \approx 4a_0/Z$.  Gauss's law applies whenever the
charge is enclosed inside a surface.

For electrons in the \textbf{same shell} (e.g.\ the
$2s$ and $2p$ orbitals in B, C, N), the situation is
fundamentally different: the orbitals \emph{overlap
spatially}.  One electron is not enclosed inside the
other.  Gauss screening is the wrong model.

\noindent\textbf{1. The EE analog: coupled microstrip.}

In electrical engineering, when two transmission
lines run through the \emph{same} dielectric medium
(neither enclosed by the other), they are modelled
as \textbf{coupled lines} with even and odd mode
impedances:
\begin{equation}
    Z_{\rm even} = Z_0 \sqrt{\frac{1+k}{1-k}},
    \qquad
    Z_{\rm odd} = Z_0 \sqrt{\frac{1-k}{1+k}}
    \label{eq:coupled_line_zo_ze}
\end{equation}
where $k$ is the mutual coupling coefficient from
electromagnetic field overlap.  The key property:
neither line ``screens'' the other --- both lines
experience a \emph{modified impedance} due to the
presence of the other.

This is a standard, well-understood formalism.
It appears at three scales in the AVE framework:

The atomic instantiation is identical: two
electron orbitals running through the same
nuclear field are coupled by mutual Coulomb
repulsion.

\noindent\textbf{2. Same-shell coupling coefficient.}

The coupling between two electrons in the same
shell (at radius $R_n = n^2 a_0 / Z$) but different
angular momentum states follows from Op4:
\begin{equation}
    k_{\rm same} = \frac{V_{\rm rep}}{E_0}
    = \frac{\alpha\hbar c \; / \; \langle r_{12} \rangle}
           {Z_{\rm eff}^2 \, R_y \, / \, n^2}
    \label{eq:k_same_shell}
\end{equation}
where $\langle r_{12} \rangle$ is the mean
inter-electron distance on the shell
(Axiom~2, Coulomb integral) and $Z_{\rm eff}$
is the effective charge after cross-shell screening.

For same-$n$, different-$l$ orbitals (e.g.\
$2s + 2p$ in B): the overlap integral is
\emph{partial} --- the $2s$ density is spherically
symmetric while the $2p$ density is axially
concentrated.  The coupling is therefore
\textbf{weaker} than for same-$l$ same-$n$ pairs
(the Hopf-link case).

If one naively models $2s^2$ as a Gauss screen for

\subparagraph{(E2k) Complete phase integral:

The fundamental problem with E2d--E2j is
architectural: the ABCD cascade finds the
eigenvalue from a phase integral
$\oint k(r)\,dr = n\pi$, but Op2 crossings
and same-shell coupling are applied
\emph{after} the eigenvalue as multiplicative
corrections.  These corrections change the
energy but do not enter the potential $V(r)$
that determines $k(r)$.  The soliton's action
does not minimise through them.

In AVE, the ground state is the configuration
where the phase integral exactly satisfies
the standing-wave condition ($B_{\text{total}}
= 0$, Op6).  \textbf{Every} interaction that
changes the soliton's energy must appear inside
$V(r)$, so that the phase integral accounts
for it.

\noindent\textbf{Key distinction:} The Gauss CDF
$\sigma_k(r)$ and the Hopf link back-EMF are
\emph{independent} interactions at different scales.
The CDF modifies the \emph{potential} $V(r)$
(capacitive, orbital scale, Regime~I linear).
The Hopf link modifies the \emph{propagation
constant} $k(r)$ (inductive, knot scale projected
to orbital, Regime~II nonlinear).  In the lumped
regime (He), these combine into a single coupling
coefficient $k_{\text{pair}}$; in the distributed
regime ($Z \geq 3$), they must be separated.

\noindent\textbf{Linking vs.\ crossing.}
Two same-$l$ solitons (identical winding numbers)
have intersection number $I = 0$---they do not
cross on the torus surface.  Instead they form
a \emph{Hopf link}: each flux tube threads
through the other's poloidal loop.  This
topological linking creates mutual inductance
$M$ (Op7, back-EMF), not a crossing potential
$V_{\rm cross}$ (Op2+Op4).  Two different-$l$
solitons (e.g.\ $2s$--$2p$, $I = 2$)
\emph{do} cross on the surface, creating
localised $V_{\rm cross}$ shunts.

\noindent\textbf{2. Complete effective potential
and propagation constant.}

The cascade eigenvalue is $B_{\rm total}(E) = 0$
(Op6), where $k(r)$ includes \emph{all}
interactions:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
    k^2(r) = \frac{2 m_e}{\hbar^2}\;
    \frac{E - V_{\rm eff}(r)}{1 + \kappa_{\rm Hopf}(r)}
}
    \label{eq:k_complete}
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
    V_{\rm eff}(r) =
    \underbrace{-\frac{Z\,\alpha\hbar c}{r}}_{\text{nuclear (Ax.~2)}}
    + \underbrace{\sum_k \frac{N_k\,\sigma_k(r)\,
      \alpha\hbar c}{r}}_{\text{screening (Op3, all shells)}}
    + \underbrace{V_{\rm cross}(r)}_{\text{crossings (Op2+Op4)}}
    + \underbrace{\frac{l(l+1)\hbar^2}{2 m_e r^2}}_{\text{angular (Op1)}}
}
    \label{eq:V_complete}
\end{equation}
and the Hopf link back-EMF coupling:
\begin{equation}
    \kappa_{\rm Hopf}(r) =
    N_{\rm Hopf} \times \frac{P_C}{2} \times (-1)^{n - l - 1}
    \times \sigma_{\rm partner}(r)
    \label{eq:kappa_hopf}
\end{equation}
where $N_{\rm Hopf}$ is the number of same-$l$
Hopf-linked partners, $P_C/2$ is the Op2
saturation at the linking point (Axiom~4),
and $\sigma_{\rm partner}(r)$ is the partner's
enclosed-charge fraction (the same CDF used for
screening, but here governing the spatial extent
of the inductive coupling).

\noindent Each term:
\begin{itemize}
