\begin{itemize}
  \item $\nu_e$ mode: impedance modified by charged-current interaction

\section{Supernova Core Collapse: Macroscopic Dielectric Rupture}
\label{sec:supernova_collapse}

The regime classification in Table~\ref{tab:stellar_regimes} terminates at neutron stars ($\varepsilon_{11} = 1.46$, Regime~IV). What happens physically when a massive stellar core crosses the Regime~III/IV boundary?

\paragraph{The AVE Mechanism.}
In the AVE framework, stellar core collapse is a macroscopic realisation of Axiom~4 (Saturation). As gravitational compression drives the core density upward, the local lattice strain $\Delta S$ increases monotonically. When $\Delta S$ exceeds the saturation plateau $V_{yield}$ across a macroscopic volume, the vacuum LC lattice undergoes \textbf{bulk dielectric rupture}---the same physics that defines particle boundaries at the subatomic scale (Vol.~2, Ch.~1), but occurring simultaneously across $\sim 10^{57}$ lattice cells.

\paragraph{The Shockwave as Lattice Relaxation.}
The rupture event is discontinuous. The instant the core lattice fractures:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The stored elastic potential energy in the over-compressed lattice is released catastrophically.
    \item The surrounding unsaturated lattice (Regime~I/II) cannot absorb this energy quasi-statically; it propagates outward as a mechanical shockwave.
    \item The shockwave velocity is set by the local impedance gradient $Z(r)$ of the stellar envelope (Section~\ref{sec:stellar_interior}), explaining why the shock stalls and must be revived by neutrino heating in the standard picture---the impedance mismatch at the tachocline-like density discontinuity partially reflects the outgoing pulse.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Neutron Star Remnant.}
The post-collapse remnant sits at $\varepsilon_{11} \approx 1.46$---deep in Regime~IV, where the lattice topology is permanently ruptured. The neutron star is therefore a macroscopic topological defect: a region where the vacuum LC lattice has undergone irreversible plastic deformation beyond the elastic yield point. Its extreme density, rapid rotation, and intense magnetic field are all consequences of the ruptured topology, computed from the same impedance operators used throughout this volume.
