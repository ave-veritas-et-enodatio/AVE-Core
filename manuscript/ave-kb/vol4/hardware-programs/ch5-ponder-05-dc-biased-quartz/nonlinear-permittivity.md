## Rarefaction Wave Inversion

Near $V_{yield}$, the nonlinear $\varepsilon_{eff}(V)$ curve creates a velocity asymmetry. The local phase velocity of acoustic perturbations depends on the instantaneous voltage:

> **[Resultbox]** *Nonlinear Acoustic Phase Velocity*
>
> $$
> c_{phase}(V) \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{\varepsilon_{eff}(V)}} \propto \left(1 - \left(\frac{V}{V_{yield}}\right)^2\right)^{1/4}
> $$

At the positive peak of the AC cycle ($V_{DC} + V_{ac}$), the phase velocity is *slower* than at the negative peak ($V_{DC} - V_{ac}$). This velocity asymmetry causes the wave crest to lag relative to the trough---the exact opposite mechanism of standard forward acoustic shockwaves.

Rather than steepening into a compressive shock front, the topological wave stretches out into a **rarefaction fan** (a backward-breaking wave). Because the steepening evaluates to a fraction less than 1, any nonlinear excess forward thrust previously derived from this mechanism is inverted. Further research is required to determine if a continuous rarefaction wave drawn through a dielectric lattice produces unidirectional macro-thrust, or if it acts as a reverse-thrust drag brake on the PONDER system.
