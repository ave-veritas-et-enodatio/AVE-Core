## Gravitomagnetism: Frame Dragging as Mutual Inductance

In standard General Relativity, a rotating massive body drags the geometric fabric of spacetime along with it---a phenomenon known as the Lense-Thirring effect (Gravitomagnetism). In the AVE framework, because the physical vacuum operates dynamically as an LC network ($\mathcal{M}_A$), this effect is analytically identical to classical **Macroscopic Mutual Inductance**.

As a massive macroscopic boundary (a spinning planet) rotates, its circulating current ($I_{mass}$) couples to the adjacent spatial metric layer via **macroscopic mutual inductance** ($M_{12}$). Macroscopic inductive momentum transport continues across the lattice, inducing a localised, steady-state magnetic bias field in the surrounding vacuum. For a 2D equatorial slice, the steady-state induced rotational vector potential of the LC lattice decays as an inverse square ($\Omega_{\text{induced}} \propto 1/r^2$). This matches the weak-field General Relativistic prediction ($\Omega_{LT}$), deriving Gravitomagnetism as standard macroscopic mutual inductance without invoking additional geometrical abstractions.

Furthermore, this inductive drag directly induces a circulating fluid velocity field ($\vec{v}$) within the spatial matrix. According to **Bernoulli's Principle**, the superposition of this bulk kinetic flow upon a propagating transverse wave creates an asymmetric dynamic pressure gradient. Consequently, wave propagation relative to the bulk flow exhibits a differential speed ($c' = c/n \pm \vec{v}$). This strictly classical aerodynamic shift is the physical cause of the asymmetric D-shaped photon capture shadows observed around rapidly rotating sources (such as Kerr black holes). The GR concept of "frame dragging" is fully isomorphic to continuous Bernoulli fluid dynamics.
