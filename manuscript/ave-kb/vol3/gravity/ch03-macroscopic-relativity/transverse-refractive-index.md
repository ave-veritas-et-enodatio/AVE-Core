## Deriving the Refractive Index

The macroscopic vacuum moduli are elevated to rank-2 symmetric tensors. Because the vacuum acts macroscopically as a Trace-Reversed Continuum to support transverse EM waves, it possesses a fixed effective Poisson ratio of $\nu_{vac} = 2/7$.

A localised massive defect does not exert a uniform 3D hydrostatic compression; it exerts a radial pull, acting as the continuous source of the principal radial tensile strain ($\epsilon_{11} > 0$). Conversely, light propagates as a transverse shear wave and couples to the orthogonal transverse spatial metric ($h_{\perp}$).

In continuum mechanics, radial tension causes orthogonal transverse space to contract ($h_{\perp} = -\nu_{vac} \cdot \epsilon_{11}$). However, the effective refractive index ($n$) scales proportionally with the physical geometric optical density ($\rho_{opt}$) of the medium. Because optical density scales inversely with physical transverse spatial displacement ($n \propto \frac{1}{1 + h_{\perp}}$), applying the first-order Taylor expansion for small macroscopic strains ($\frac{1}{1+x} \approx 1-x$):

Therefore, a negative (compressive) transverse strain yields a positive increase in the effective refractive index:

> **[Resultbox]** *Transverse Refractive Index*
>
> $$
> n(r) = 1 - h_{\perp} = 1 - (-\nu_{vac}\epsilon_{11}) = 1 + \nu_{vac}\epsilon_{11}
> $$
