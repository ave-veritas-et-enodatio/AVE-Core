## Interior Topology and Singularity Resolution

In classical General Relativity, matter falling into a black hole accelerates indefinitely toward a point singularity at $r = 0$, leading to infinite curvature and infinite physical density.

In the AVE topological framework, the event horizon marks the boundary of the elastic lattice, governed by the principal radial strain $\varepsilon_{11}(r) = 7\,GM/(c^2 r)$. At the saturation boundary ($r_{sat} = 3.5\,r_s$), we reach $\varepsilon_{11} = 1$, and the lattice undergoes a phase transition where the shear modulus $G_{shear} \to 0$.

The classical "infinite collapse" is halted deterministically via the Topo-Relativistic impedance divergence. The effective inertial density of collapsing matter diverges as the topological yield approaches zero:

> **[Resultbox]** *Topo-Relativistic Impedance Divergence*
>
> $$
> \rho_{eff} = \frac{\rho_0}{S_{topo}^3}
> $$
> where $S_{topo}(r) = \sqrt{1 - \varepsilon_{11}^2}$

As infalling matter approaches $r_{sat}$, $\rho_{eff} \to \infty$ relative to the saturated boundary metric. The group velocity of the collapsing mass $c_g \to 0$, causing a **Topological Halting**. 

Radial collapse into a point is physically forbidden. The structure freezes exactly at the phase transition boundary, forming a hollow or densely compact spherical shell stabilized at $r \approx r_{sat}$. The singularity is therefore the phase-transition limit of the lattice's capacity to transport topological strain, resolved with zero phenomenological smoothing parameters.
