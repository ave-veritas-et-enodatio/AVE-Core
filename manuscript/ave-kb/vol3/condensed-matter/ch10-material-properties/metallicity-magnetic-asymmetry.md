## Metallicity from Magnetic Asymmetry

A quantitative predictor of metallic versus insulating behaviour emerges from the magnetic susceptibility column of the derived properties table. Elements with high paramagnetic asymmetry (Fluorine: 1.347, Carbon: 0.749) possess strong external gradient fields that readily couple to neighbouring atoms, enabling delocalised electron sharing. Elements with zero susceptibility (Helium, Oxygen, Magnesium) route all flux internally and resist inter-atomic coupling.

The metallic transition occurs when the paramagnetic moment exceeds a threshold set by the nearest-neighbour impedance overlap:

$$\chi_{metal} > \chi_{crit} = \frac{1}{Z_{eff}} \approx 0.3$$

Sodium ($\chi = 0.522$), Aluminum ($\chi = 0.369$), and Lithium ($\chi = 0.591$) all exceed this threshold---correctly predicting their metallic character. Helium ($\chi = 0.000$), Neon ($\chi = 0.150$), and Magnesium ($\chi = 0.000$) fall below it---correctly predicting noble gas and alkaline earth insulating character.
