When Boron ($2p^1$) emerges, its ionization energy drops precipitously from Beryllium ($9.32$ eV) to $8.30$ eV, universally confounding classical models.

The outer polar track *never physically intersects* the inner core track. Phase 5 collisions operate exclusively between elements sharing the same $R_n$ macroscopic bounding cavity.

Every time a wave crosses an impedance junction at an angle $\theta$, a fraction of energy is scattered from the standing wave path:

$$
Y_{\text{loss}} = c_{\text{total}} \frac{1 - \cos\theta}{2\pi^2}
$$

where $c_{\text{total}}$ is the topological number of physical crossing junctions on the path. There are two fundamental intersection regimes:

1. **Hopf Coupling (Parallel Plane):** Two electrons mapped to the exact same projection plane (e.g., $2s^1 \rightarrow 2s^2$) lock into a $\pi$-antiphase dipole. They physically cross exactly 2 times per orbit (at the geometric poles). Thus, $c=2$ with angle $\theta = \pi$.

2. **Cross-Subshell (Orthogonal Plane):** An electron constrained to an orthogonal polar track (e.g., $2p_1$) must slice through the equatorial track ($2s$). For every equatorial partner, the polar track clips it twice (climbing from the South node, and diving from the North node). Thus, $c=4$ per orthogonal partner, with angle $\theta = \pi/2$.

Because this is a multiplicative drop in dynamic cavity pressure, the total transmission $T = 1 - Y_{\text{loss}}$. For the quadratic (energy) eigenvalue, the total drain is $f_{\text{drain}} = (T_{\pi} T_{\pi/2})^2$.
